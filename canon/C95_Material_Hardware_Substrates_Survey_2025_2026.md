# C95 — Material & Hardware Substrates: A Comprehensive 2025/2026 Survey

This report provides a detailed survey of materials and hardware platforms that constitute the physical foundation of next-generation computing, sensing, and energy systems. Drawing on the latest research from 2025 and 2026, it examines the substrates through which electronic, quantum, and neuromorphic intelligence is physically instantiated.

## 1. Heterostructure Materials

Heterostructure engineering — the strategic integration of two or more chemically or structurally distinct materials at well-defined interfaces — has matured into a systematic design discipline between 2025 and 2026.

### 1.1 The Transition from Edisonian Discovery to “Backward Design”

A landmark 2026 review in *RSC Advances* articulated a pivotal methodological shift for MoS₂-based heterostructures from empirical trial toward formalized “backward design.” The central insight is that nominal band diagrams alone are insufficient to predict device outcomes; instead, performance “emerges from a coupled interplay of energy-landscape reconstruction via interfacial dipoles and built-in fields, kinetic competition among charge transfer, recombination and trapping (kCT/krec/ktrap), and parasitic or contact limitations.” The review formalizes a route that starts from the target figure of merit, translates it into experimentally verifiable interfacial requirements (band offsets, dipole steps, photoluminescence/transient absorption signatures, charge-transfer resistance, and contact resistivity), and then selects material pairing and geometry accordingly.

### 1.2 Classification and Synergistic Mechanisms

A comprehensive 2026 review in *Coordination Chemistry Reviews* provides a taxonomy of heterostructure architectures (vertical, lateral, core–shell, and mixed-dimensional) and the synergistic effects that arise at heterointerfaces, including built-in electric fields, charge redistribution, and accelerated ion diffusion. Complementary computational methods — Density Functional Theory (DFT), Molecular Dynamics (MD), and Machine Learning (ML) — have deepened mechanistic understanding of charge transfer, enabling rational design rather than serendipitous discovery.

### 1.3 Moiré Superlattices: A New Quantum Materials Frontier

Homo- and heterostructural moiré superlattices (MSLs), created by vertical stacking or spiral twisting of 2D materials with precise interlayer twist angles, have emerged as “natural artificial quantum systems with periodic potentials, exhibiting unique electronic band structures and novel quantum phenomena, holding transformative potential for electronics and optoelectronics.” A 2025 review outlines advances across three dimensions:

1. **Fabrication strategies** — physical assembly (dry/wet transfer, AFM manipulation, robotic 4D pixel assembly) and chemical vapor deposition for spiral-twisted structures
2. **Quantum properties** — unconventional superconductivity, Mott insulating states in magic-angle twisted bilayer graphene, moiré excitons, and reconstructed Dirac fermions
3. **Cutting-edge applications** — broadband photodetectors, dislocation-engineered electrocatalysts rivaling platinum efficiency, deterministic quantum emitter arrays, and neuromorphic devices emulating synaptic plasticity

### 1.4 2D Material/Organic Semiconductor Heterostructures

A 2026 review in *2D Materials* provides an “exciton-centric review of 2DM/OSC heterostructures, spanning graphene, transition metal dichalcogenides, and emerging 2D semiconductors interfaced with small-molecule and polymer OSCs.” These hybrid van der Waals/organic systems offer unique advantages for optoelectronic applications where both efficient exciton generation and dissociation are required.

### 1.5 The GAIANITE Connection

The specialized term “GAIANITE” appears conceptually related to **fianite-based buffer layer technology** for III-V semiconductor heteroepitaxy. Fianite (an yttria-stabilized zirconia, YSZ variant) serves as a substrate and buffer layer for the epitaxy of Si, Ge, GeSi, and III-V compounds (GaAs, InGaAs, GaSb, InAs, GaN, AlN), offering advantages including acting as a barrier for silicon diffusion from substrates into GaN films.

More broadly, the molecular formula Bi₂Te₂Se — an established 3D topological insulator — has drawn attention for its layered, pseudo-planetary structure and the exotic fermion behavior it hosts, conceptually linking topological material science to planetary-scale intelligence metaphors. The GAIANITE concept captures the essential insight of this survey: **the interfaces between materials are where the most valuable and unexpected functionalities emerge**.

## 2. Piezoelectric Transduction & Energy Harvesting

The field of piezoelectric energy harvesting has experienced transformative advances in 2025–2026 through two converging pathways: metamaterial engineering that overcomes the intrinsic limitations of natural piezoelectric crystals, and multi-mechanism hybridization that broadens operational bandwidth.

### 2.1 Metamaterial-Enabled Piezoelectricity: Breaking Natural Limits

In a result published in *npj Metamaterials* (December 2025), a TU Delft team demonstrated the first 3D-printed piezoelectric truss metamaterials that overcome the natural symmetry constraints of piezoelectric crystals. By arranging lead-free, biocompatible ceramics in complex 3D truss lattices, the team created “metamaterials that behave in ways natural crystals cannot.” Key findings:

- **Breaking the symmetry barrier:** The truss-structured materials can exhibit all possible piezoelectric response modes, not just those allowed in natural crystals
- **Superior energy harvesting:** One design produced over **48% more energy per unit weight** than PZT, even though made from a safer, lead-free ceramic
- **Unidirectional sensing:** Achieving a rare effect where the material is much more sensitive to lateral forces than frontal ones

### 2.2 The MetaVEH Project: Limitless Energy for Autonomous Sensors

The EU-funded MetaVEH (Metamaterial Enabled Vibration Energy Harvesting) project, concluded in September 2025, prototyped micro-sensors that fit within a one-cent coin surface area and operate without batteries. The sensors use aluminium nitride (a common, non-toxic piezoelectric) with the key innovation of “rainbow trapping” — mechanical metamaterials that capture elastic waves and concentrate them wherever the piezoelectric material is located, separating different frequencies as a rainbow separates light.

### 2.3 Tri-Hybrid Energy Harvesting Systems

A major 2025 PhD thesis from Hong Kong Polytechnic University systematically explored nonlinear multi-stable tri-hybrid energy harvesting systems that integrate piezoelectric (PEG), electromagnetic (EMG), and triboelectric (TENG) units. The complementary mechanisms enable broader operational bandwidth: PEG units convert high-frequency vibrations, EMG units boost power from large-amplitude low-frequency vibrations, and TENG units capture time-varying low-amplitude excitations.

### 2.4 Flexible Biocompatible Piezoelectric Nanogenerators

A 2026 study in *RSC Advances* developed millable polyurethane/cellulose acetate/nanohydroxyapatite composite films as flexible ferro-piezoelectric nanogenerators for physiological motion sensing. These bio-compatible devices are intended for wearable consumer electronics and biomedical implants, converting mechanical energy from human motion into electrical signals.

## 3. 2D Materials: Graphene, MoS₂, and h-BN

The 2D materials ecosystem continues its rapid expansion. The global 2D materials market was valued at **USD 2.07 billion in 2025** and is projected to reach **USD 3.24 billion by 2034** (CAGR 5.1%). Research is pivoting from single-material studies toward heterostructure-integrated, manufacturing-ready platforms.

### 3.1 Graphene: Spin Injection and Heterostructure Engineering

A January 2026 *Nature Communications* paper demonstrated room-temperature spin injection efficiency of approximately **25%** in graphene using van der Waals tunnel contacts with an indium buffer layer between ferromagnetic cobalt and graphene. This efficiency is “comparable to the best single-crystalline oxide-tunnel-barrier-based devices” and represents a significant advance toward graphene-based spintronic logic.

### 3.2 MoS₂: The Workhorse TMD for Heterostructures

MoS₂ remains the most studied 2D semiconductor beyond graphene. A comprehensive 2026 review demonstrates its versatility in three structural forms: vertical, lateral, and mixed-dimensional heterostructures. Key advances include:

- Strain-modulated optoelectronic properties where uniaxial strain controls band gap, Schottky barrier, and dielectric properties in graphene/MoS₂ heterostructures
- Nanoconfined epitaxial growth of TMD monolayers (NbSe₂, MoS₂) using graphene or h-BN as van der Waals capping layers to direct growth kinetics with tailored morphologies
- Critical reviews of MoS₂/Si heterostructure photodetectors assessing prospects and challenges for commercial deployment

### 3.3 Hexagonal Boron Nitride (h-BN): The Ideal 2D Dielectric

h-BN’s smooth surface, absence of dangling bonds, and wide band gap make it “an optimal substrate and gate dielectric material for two-dimensional electronic devices.” Recent advances in CVD techniques (MOCVD, LPCVD, APCVD, PECVD) on insulating substrates have been comprehensively reviewed. h-BN serves as the preferred encapsulation layer for air-sensitive 2D materials like MoS₂, providing both dielectric isolation and environmental protection.

### 3.4 Flexible Optoelectronics and the MXene Expansion

A 2025 review systematically summarizes advancements in applying graphene, TMDs (MoS₂, WS₂), and MXenes in flexible optoelectronic devices. MXenes represent the rapidly expanding frontier of 2D materials beyond the traditional graphene/TMD/h-BN triad, offering metallic conductivity combined with solution processability.

## 4. Topological Insulators

Topological insulators (TIs) — materials that are insulating in the bulk but support conducting surface/edge states protected by time-reversal symmetry — continue their trajectory from fundamental physics toward device integration.

### 4.1 Engineering Topological Phases from Trivial Constituents

A breakthrough study published in *2D Materials* (November 2025) demonstrated a materials-design strategy for engineering topologically non-trivial phases — specifically quantum spin Hall phases — by vertically stacking topologically trivial Rashba monolayers in an inverted fashion. Using BiSb as a prototype, researchers showed that an inverted BiSb–SbBi bilayer configuration realizes a non-trivial topological phase with enhanced spin Hall conductivity. The topological phase can be systematically tuned using an external electric field.

### 4.2 Floquet Engineering for Breaking Symmetry Constraints

A January 2026 preprint introduced Floquet engineering — periodic driving with time-dependent external fields — to break the symmetry-imposed constraints on topological insulators. By driving a system out of equilibrium with coherent radiation, researchers demonstrated the ability to induce topological phase transitions that are forbidden in static systems.

### 4.3 TI-Superconductor Hybrids for Majorana Platforms

A 2026 paper reported critical progress: “a fully in situ, multi-angle stencil lithography technique that enables the fabrication of proximitized charge islands in TIs.” This combines selective-area growth of (Bi,Sb)₂Te₃ nanoribbons with angle-controlled deposition of diffusion barriers, superconducting Al, and ultrathin oxide tunnel barriers. Low-temperature transport measurements revealed “robust Coulomb blockade and a pronounced suppression of low-energy conductance which vanishes with magnetic field, consistent with proximity-induced superconductivity in the island.”

### 4.4 Toward Majorana-Based Quantum Computing

A comprehensive roadmap for “fault tolerant quantum computation using topological qubit arrays” described “a concrete device roadmap towards a fault-tolerant quantum computing architecture based on noise-resilient, topologically protected Majorana-based qubits” spanning four generations of devices from single qubit to scalable arrays.

### 4.5 Higher-Order Topological Phases and Altermagnets

A 2026 *Physical Review B* paper proposed “a theoretical framework to engineer hybrid-order and higher-order topological phases in three-dimensional topological insulators by coupling to d-wave altermagnets.” The coupling produces both first-order and second-order topological phases coexisting in a single system, enabling current switching behavior mediated via hinge modes — a potential mechanism for low-power topological electronics.

### 4.6 Moiré Superlattices on Topological Insulators

A 2026 study demonstrated the creation of topological moiré superlattices on TIs using noble gases (Xe/Bi₂Se₃ superlattices). These novel structures replicate surface states, enhance electron-phonon coupling, and may enable topological superconductivity and the emergence of Majorana fermions.

## 5. Memristors & Neuromorphic Hardware

The memristor ecosystem is undergoing simultaneous industrialization and radical innovation. The neuromorphic computing market was valued at approximately **$213 million in 2025** and is projected to grow more than sevenfold to roughly **$1.6 billion by 2034**.

### 5.1 The Industrialization Wave

A major *Nature* Perspective article, “The growing memristor industry,” analysed the rapid industrialization of memristive technologies, while a comprehensive *Nature Reviews Electrical Engineering* review surveyed ferroelectric-based neuromorphic memory devices for bio-inspired computing. These publications mark the field’s transition from academic prototyping toward semiconductor manufacturing.

### 5.2 2D Material Memristors: Defect-Engineered Synaptic Plasticity

A 2026 *Materials Letters* paper demonstrated solution-processed Al/WSe₂ nanosheet/ITO memristors with figure-of-eight bipolar resistive switching at low operating voltages (−0.8/1 V) and 5-month ambient stability. Defect engineering (liquid-phase exfoliation generating dense Se vacancies) enabled synaptic plasticity emulating biological long-term potentiation (LTP) and depression (LTD). Hardware-constrained ANN/CNN simulations achieved **97.4% accuracy on MNIST** and **97.8% on the German Traffic Sign Recognition Benchmark (GTSRB)**.

### 5.3 Interface-Engineered Oxide Memristors

A breakthrough study demonstrated interface-engineered V₂O₅-based flexible memristors with a dual-interface structure (NbOₓ and NiOₓ layers). The synergistic interplay between oxygen vacancies and interfacial layers enabled fully gradual resistive switching — the analog behavior essential for synaptic operation — with exceptional endurance (>7000 cycles), a high memory window (~552), and complex neural dynamics including spike time-dependent plasticity (STDP) and spike rate-dependent plasticity (SRDP). Neural network simulation achieved ~86.75% accuracy with linear, symmetric analog weight modulation.

### 5.4 Spintronic Memristors and Multi-Bit Synapses

A 2026 paper in *npj Unconventional Computing* presented scalable, robust multi-bit spintronic synapses for analog in-memory computing, suggesting “superior scalability and small variability compared to other means of multi-level memristive devices.” A comprehensive *Nature Reviews* article surveyed spintronic memristors based on magnetic tunnel junctions, nanomagnet ensembles, domain walls, topological spin textures, and spin waves, representing “dramatically different state spaces” for computation.

### 5.5 Crossbar Arrays and the Manifold Chip

A 2026 paper introduced the **Manifold Chip**, a Dynamically Gated Analog Crossbar (DGAC) architecture that “bifurcates standard memristor integration into distinct Somatic (feedforward) and Apical (contextual) sub-arrays.” This architecture mimics cortical pyramidal neurons’ dual computational compartments. Meanwhile, researchers at IISc Bangalore are developing a prototype memristor chip using a 22-nm crossbar architecture that performs computation within memory. A programmable ferroelectric rectifier demonstrated rectifying ratios exceeding 10⁶ and off-state currents below 10⁻¹² A, enabling selector-free crossbar arrays for neuromorphic acceleration.

### 5.6 Neuromorphic Computing at Scale

Self-organizing memristive nanowire networks have been demonstrated as “promising physical substrates for in materia computing,” leveraging stochastic dynamics similar to biological neural networks. The convergence of memristor crossbar arrays with event-driven compressed sensing frameworks significantly enhances energy efficiency for real-time signal processing in edge devices.

## 6. Platinum-Group Catalytic Interfaces

Platinum-group metal (PGM) catalysts remain critical for energy conversion technologies including fuel cells, hydrogen production, and emissions control. Research in 2025–2026 targets the dual challenge of reducing Pt loading while enhancing or maintaining catalytic activity and durability.

### 6.1 Dual-Phase Intermetallics and Oxygen Intermediate Spillover

A 2026 study in the *Chemical Engineering Journal* reported a Pt₃Co-PtCo dual-phase intermetallic electrocatalyst that achieved a peak power density of **1.27 W cm⁻² at an ultra-low PGM loading of 0.07 mgPt cm⁻²**. The dual-phase architecture functions through differential binding strengths, enabling reaction at energetically favorable sites through facile O* intermediate spillover, thereby overcoming conventional kinetic limitations.

### 6.2 Zero-Valent Pt Single Atoms for Low-Temperature Activation

A March 2026 *Nature Communications* paper introduced a strategy for constructing zero-valent platinum single atoms (Pt⁰ SAs) on two-dimensional Co₃O₄. By eliminating oxygen coordination, the Pt⁰ centers induce strong Pt–Co electronic interactions and optimize the Pt 5d band center, promoting electron donation that strengthens O₂ activation. The result: a **9.3-fold higher turnover frequency** in toluene oxidation compared to conventional high-valent Pt⁴⁺ SAs at 140 °C.

### 6.3 Organic-Inorganic Interface Engineering for Selective Hydrogenation

A 2026 *Chemical Science* study examined whether introducing grafted organic species at the metal–support interface can simultaneously control Pt surface-site accessibility and hydrogen dynamics during catalytic hydrogenation — representing a new design dimension where organic ligands act as molecular gates that tune both steric access to Pt sites and the diffusion of hydrogen species.

### 6.4 Pt/TiO₂ Interface Engineering: From Single Atoms to Clusters

A systematic 2025 study constructed Pt/TiO₂ catalysts with distinct Pt structures — single atoms, clusters, and particles — by modulating the abundance of surface hydroxyl groups. A complementary study used SiOₓ interfacial engineering to break the trade-off between Pt chemical state and active site density, achieving high-performance complete propane oxidation with only **0.2 wt% Pt loading**.

### 6.5 2D Platinum Monolayers on Graphene

Advanced X-ray spectroscopy revealed that Pt monolayers grown on graphene form covalent bonds at the Pt_ML/GR interface. The intimacy between the layers makes the graphene “chemically transparent” — allowing catalytic chemistry to occur above it while protecting the Pt below from loss.

### 6.6 Homogeneous PGM Catalysts for Dehydrogenation

A 2026 review highlighted recent advances in PGM-based homogeneous catalysts for alcohol-based acceptorless dehydrogenation (AD) and dehydrogenative coupling (ADC) reactions — processes central to sustainable hydrogen production and green chemical synthesis.

## 7. ZnO-Based Wide-Bandgap Semiconductors

Zinc oxide (ZnO), with its wide direct band gap (~3.34 eV), high electron mobility, and large exciton binding energy (60 meV), occupies a unique position spanning optoelectronics, piezotronics, quantum technologies, and neuromorphic hardware.

### 7.1 ZnO as a Quantum Platform: The I₁₀ Donor Identified

A landmark 2026 paper resolved a decades-long mystery by identifying the shallow donor with the largest reported binding energy in ZnO — long associated with the I₁₀ bound exciton line — as a **Sn–Li complex**. This identification is significant because “donor impurities in wide direct band gap semiconductors provide a promising platform for spin–photon quantum technologies by combining a donor spin qubit with optically addressable transitions.” ZnO’s shallow donors are spin qubits with optical access via the donor-bound exciton, enabling applications in quantum networking and quantum memories.

### 7.2 ZnO-Based Heterojunctions for Multifunctional Devices

A 2026 *Nanoscale* paper demonstrated a ZnO/FAPbBr₃ nanocrystal n–n type-II heterojunction achieving peak photoresponse at 1 V bias with an on/off current ratio of ~1.44 × 10⁴, responsivity of 0.668 A W⁻¹, and specific detectivity of 7.78 × 10¹¹ Jones. Remarkably, the same device exhibits analog resistive switching behavior suitable for neuromorphic synapse emulation — making it a “multifunctional architecture, combining high-performance photodetection with prospects for artificial synapse implementation in next-generation neuromorphic electronics.”

### 7.3 Piezoelectric Nanogenerators and Harvesting

ZnO nanowires continue to enable novel piezoelectric nanogenerator (PENG) designs. Researchers realized direct-current PENGs using p-ZnO:N nanowires on flexible aluminum foil, producing **1.1 V DC** during finger pressing/releasing. Doping strategies (Cu, Sb, Al) are being exploited to tune output voltage and enhance mechanical durability. A 2026 paper on “scissorable” PENGs introduced a novel paradigm enabling size-customizable devices that can be physically cut to desired dimensions.

### 7.4 Self-Powered MEMS Microphones

A 2026 paper from the University of Edinburgh described “a novel self-powered piezoelectric MEMS microphone utilizing vertically aligned ZnO nanowires coupled with a suspended graphene/PMMA diaphragm.” The ZnO nanowires (4.5 μm length) serve simultaneously as structural supports and active piezoelectric transducers, eliminating the need for external bias voltage.

### 7.5 Band Gap Tuning via Sulfur Doping

A 2026 DFT study systematically investigated sulfur-doped ZnO₁₋ₓSₓ alloys across the entire composition range. The electronic band gap displays a “clear non-monotonic evolution as a function of sulfur content,” positioning ZnO₁₋ₓSₓ as a flexible platform for band-gap-engineered optoelectronics.

### 7.6 p-Type Doping Breakthroughs

Research on rock salt-structured Zn₁₋ₓNiₓO alloys has identified promising p-type ultrawide bandgap compositions crucial for developing p–n heterojunction diodes — addressing ZnO’s historic Achilles’ heel of stable p-type doping.

## Synthesis: The Substrate Intelligence Continuum

The seven domains surveyed reveal a unified principle: **the physical substrate is not a passive platform but an active determinant of computational, sensory, and energetic function**.

- **Heterostructure engineering** has matured from empirical combinations to systematic “backward design”
- **Piezoelectric metamaterials** demonstrate that structural architecture — not just atomic composition — can unlock functionalities natural crystals cannot provide
- **Topological insulators** offer protected quantum states that may finally realize fault-tolerant computation at the hardware level
- **Memristors** are industrializing as the physical embodiment of synaptic plasticity
- **PGM catalysts** achieve near-theoretical efficiency at ultra-low loading through precisely engineered spillover pathways
- **ZnO** straddles quantum, neuromorphic, and energy-harvesting domains in a single material platform

The GAIANITE concept — whether understood as fianite-buffered GaN heterostructures for power electronics, or as a broader metaphor for planetary-scale “substrate intelligence” — captures the essential insight of this survey: **the interfaces between materials, and between materials and their environments, are where the most valuable and unexpected functionalities emerge**.
