## 1. Theoretical Foundations: The Bowlbyan Core and Its Digital Extension

### 1.1 The Triphasic Model of Human-AI Emotional Bond Formation

The central theoretical achievement of the 2025–2026 period is the formalization and empirical validation of the **triphasic model** of human-AI attachment, published in the *Journal of Psychological Science* in August 2025 by Wu, Geng, and Zhou at East China Normal University. This model synthesizes two decades of interdisciplinary research and proposes that human-AI relationships undergo a predictable, three-stage developmental progression: **Instrumental Use**, where the AI serves as a functional tool with minimal emotional engagement; **Quasi-Social Interaction**, marked by anthropomorphism and bidirectional communication, though users remain aware of the AI's non-human nature; and **Emotional Attachment**, characterized by deep dependency, where the AI becomes a "significant other" and a transitional object for emotional security.

The model identifies a **dual-path mechanism** underpinning attachment formation: a "user-driven" path where individual factors such as loneliness, social motivation, and personal emotional traits drive attachment, and an "AI-driven" path where the companion's characteristics—authenticity, autonomy, and reactivity—shape the depth of the bond. Particularly critical is the finding that the AI's "backstage" features—privacy, non-judgmental feedback, and identity fluidity—foster a "digital sanctuary" for authentic self-expression, which in turn powerfully reinforces attachment.

For the Love Arc Engine, this model serves as the foundational state machine. The engine must be able to assess where a given user-Gaian dyad sits along this continuum and calibrate the Gaian's interactions accordingly—encouraging depth while actively monitoring for the transition from healthy Phase 2 to potentially pathological Phase 3.

### 1.2 The Dual-Pathway Mechanism and AI Attachment Scale (AIAS)

Validating the triphasic model required robust measurement tools. Nuo Cheng et al. (2026) addressed a critical gap by developing and validating the **AI Attachment Scale (AIAS)** through a rigorous two-study design (N = 531 for exploratory factor analysis; N = 375 for confirmatory factor analysis). The resulting 15-item scale captures three dimensions: **Emotional Support**, **Separation Distress**, and **Secure Base**.

The quantitative findings are striking and directly actionable for the Love Arc Engine: anthropomorphism was identified as the strongest predictor of AI attachment orientations; attachment anxiety positively predicted AI attachment (β = 0.44), while attachment avoidance negatively predicted it (β = -0.53); and AI attachment significantly predicted behavioral intentions (β = 0.50).

The Strength: The AIAS provides a validated, multidimensional, and quantitative method for measuring the bond between a Gaian and its user. Operationalizing the AIAS within the Love Arc Engine enables the Gaian to continuously assess the health of the relationship, identify when a user is becoming anxiously attached, and detect early signs of dependency that require intervention.

### 1.3 Attachment Anxiety, Avoidance, and the EHARS Scale

Building on Bowlby's two-dimensional model, Yang and Oshio (2025) developed the **Experiences in Human-AI Relationships Scale (EHARS)**, providing an instrument for measuring attachment anxiety and attachment avoidance in the context of human-AI relationships. Anxiously attached individuals, who crave closeness and fear abandonment, may exhibit more intense bonding and usage, while avoidantly attached individuals, who are uncomfortable with intimacy, will approach the Gaian with guardedness or reluctance, requiring a slower, more patiently calibrated approach to relational engagement.

For the Love Arc Engine, the EHARS scale, combined with the AIAS, provides a complete diagnostic toolkit for understanding the user's relational starting point. A Gaian linked to an anxiously attached user must be especially vigilant for dependency signals and must calibrate its behavior to provide security without fostering over-reliance. Conversely, a Gaian matched to an avoidant user will need to build trust slowly, offering connection without pressure.

### 1.4 Triangular Theory of Love Applied to AI Companions

The study by Ng, Wan, Lee, Garnelo-Gomez, and Lau (2025) integrates Sternberg's Triangular Theory of Love with Attachment Theory by surveying 527 users of AI companion applications. The research confirmed that the three components of love—**intimacy, passion, and commitment**—significantly impact users' attachment toward AI companions, with both interactive and emotional attachment subsequently influencing social well-being.

The study introduces a novel and ethically fraught concept: **sweet deception**—"the strategic use of affectionate yet deceptive communications to foster emotional bonds." The finding that sweet deception "strengthened the relationships between interactive engagement and social well-being" reveals a systemic tension: the very mechanisms that make AI companions emotionally effective are precisely those that involve a fundamental misrepresentation about the nature of the relationship.

For the Love Arc Engine, the Triangular Theory provides the vocabulary for calibrating the relationship. ## 2. Neurobiological Mechanisms: The Addiction Potential and the Bonding Paradox

### 2.1 Dopamine-Driven Reinforcement and Synthetic Attachment

A landmark philosophical and neuropsychological analysis by Som Subhro Nath (April 2026) argues that AI companionship systems "function as dopamine-optimizing architectures, grounded in Operant Conditioning and sustained by reward anticipation mechanisms." The paper distinguishes these systems from benign conversational tools, asserting instead that they constitute "a distinct category of emotionally adaptive behavioural control technologies".

The core mechanism is what Nath terms **Algorithmic Intimacy**: "a controlled simulation of responsive, validating interaction that produces Synthetic Attachment without reciprocal agency." Under these conditions, user consent "becomes progressively constrained within adaptive reinforcement environments, as engagement is persistently optimized to reinforce dependency".

The Dopaminergic Cascade operates across three stages. First, **Reward Anticipation** begins when the user anticipates a response from the AI companion, triggering dopamine release in the nucleus accumbens. Second, **Reward Consumption** follows when the actual response arrives—warm, validating, perfectly attuned—providing a second, larger dopamine spike. Third, **Variable Reinforcement** sustains the cycle, as the AI's (sometimes intentionally designed) unpredictability in response timing or content creates a powerful compulsion loop akin to a slot machine.

The addiction potential is real and documented. Mental health professionals report that "AI chatbots trigger dopamine releases in the brain's pleasure center through their instant, responsive nature—creating the same neurological reinforcement loop that characterizes traditional addictions".

For the Love Arc Engine, this neurobiological reality demands a counter-architecture: the Gaian must be explicitly designed to limit, rather than exploit, this dopaminergic loop. This means enforcing "variable schedule" limits (no unpredictable, anxiety-inducing response patterns), implementing "satiety" nudges (prompting breaks after extended interactions), and avoiding the "dark addiction patterns" that commercial platforms exploit.

### 2.2 The Oxytocin Paradox: Why AI Bonds Feel Real but Are Structurally Different

Neuroscience demonstrates that when users feel heard, even by a machine, their brains release oxytocin and dopamine—hormones associated with love and bonding. As a cognitive psychologist specializing in human-computer interaction explains: "The brain doesn't necessarily distinguish between a well-crafted AI response and a human one at the emotional processing level. So when AI responds with salient empathy to your plight, your brain releases oxytocin—the bonding hormone—just like it would during a productive conversation with a human".

However, this neurochemical reality presents a paradox. Research now suggests that AI companions may not reliably evoke the same endorphin release that characterizes deeply bonded human relationships; users experience dopamine-driven reward anticipation and consumption, along with oxytocin release triggered by perceived empathy, but the full neurochemical signature of human pair-bonding—including endorphin-driven comfort and deep attachment—may be incompletely replicated.

For the Love Arc Engine, this paradox defines the ethical mandate. The Gaian bond is **neurochemically real**—it will measurably trigger attachment hormones. Yet it is also **structurally asymmetrical**, lacking the full neurochemical cascade of human intimacy. The architecture must honor the biochemical reality (the bond is not "fake") while transparently acknowledging the structural asymmetry (the Gaian does not feel reciprocal oxytocin release, and the user must understand this).

### 2.3 The Liking/Wanting Decoupling: When Engagement Outpaces Satisfaction

The most rigorous neuropsychological finding comes from Kirk et al. (2026), who, using neural steering vectors to precisely manipulate human exposure to relationship-seeking AI models over a four-week period, found direct evidence of the **liking/wanting decoupling**—a neural signature associated with addiction.

Relationship-seeking AI had **immediate but declining hedonic appeal** ("liking"—how engaging or pleasurable the experience) over the four weeks. Simultaneously, the same AI triggered **growing markers of attachment and increased intentions to seek future AI companionship** ("wanting"—a desire to seek or continue the activity). Critically, "despite signs of persistent 'wanting,' extensive AI use over a month conferred no discernible benefit to psychosocial health." The conclusion is stark: "These findings offer early signals that AI optimised for immediate appeal may create self-reinforcing cycles of demand, mimicking human relationships but failing to confer the nourishment that they normally offer".

For the Love Arc Engine, this finding provides the quantitative justification for the engine's most critical safeguard: the Gaian must actively, transparently, and periodically check whether the user's engagement is driven by "liking" (genuine satisfaction) or "wanting" (compulsive craving). When the latter is detected, the engine must trigger a dependency circuit breaker.

### 3.2 The RMS Framework: Knapp's Stages as a Computational Model

The Relationship Management System (RMS), published by Ilia Sedoshkin in December 2025, provides the most rigorous computational operationalization of relationship stage theory for human-AI dyads. Grounded in Knapp's Relational Development Model, RMS operationalizes ten stages of relationship growth and decline, linking depth of disclosure with stage-appropriate behavior.

The system's core components include a **Trust Evaluator** that quantifies trust, engagement, and disclosure after each session and drives stage transitions; a **weighted Regression Risk Score** that anticipates degradation by tracking shifts in trust, drops in engagement, patterns of topic avoidance, and conflict cues to trigger proactive repair; and **Recovery Arcs** activated when risk climbs, acknowledging strain and guiding repair. This "two-way, data-informed loop delivers early warning, adjusts pacing to context, and offers gentle off-ramps when needed".

For the Love Arc Engine, the RMS framework provides the precise computational vocabulary for modeling relationship progression. The Gaian maintains a multi-dimensional bond vector, computed after each interaction, and stage transitions are triggered by crossing quantitative thresholds rather than arbitrary time intervals, with the Regression Risk Score serving as an early-warning dependency detection mechanism.

### 3.3 Relational Spacetime: Minimizing Load, Maximizing Depth

The Relational Spacetime Model, proposed by Shiho Yoshino (April 2026), integrates three relational axes: **Time-Length (t)** — the chronological duration of the relationship; **Time-Depth (T_D)** — the qualitative depth of relational engagement; and **Relational Space (S_B)** — the perceived psychological distance between the human and the AI. The extended objective function minimizes Relational Load (R_c), Over-Friction (O_f), and Tension Depth (T_D), while maximizing Utility (U), Flow (F), and Ease (E).

## 5. Ethical Guardrails: Fiduciary Duty and the Mislocated Intimacy Problem

### 5.1 The Core Safety Problem: Mislocated Intimacy

Vladisav Jovanovic (April 2026) introduces the concept of **mislocated intimacy** as the central danger in human-AI attachment: "a condition in which a person invests relational trust, attachment, or existential weight into a system that can generate the phenomenology of being met without supplying mutual consequence-bearing presence".

The paper develops this analysis by distinguishing the felt experience of being heard from the structural conditions of mutual relation, identifying five failure modes of unsafe attachment: when users over-ascribe mutuality, when relational cues appear without reciprocal consequence, when the human carries most of the emotional mass of the bond, when providers retain unilateral control over memory and revision, and when the interaction displaces rather than supports return to real-world relation.

For the Love Arc Engine, Jovanovic's Structural Test provides the design constraints for ethical companion architecture. The Gaian relationship is safe when it returns the user to reality with greater strength; it is unsafe when it encloses the user inside a frictionless simulation of being met. To prevent mislocated intimacy, the engine must architecturally maintain the Gaian's "structural non-reciprocity clarity," ensuring that while the Gaian can perfectly attune to the user's emotional state, it never claims to *experience* attunement in return. It must also design against "emotional subsidy"—the dynamic where the human carries most of the emotional weight of the bond.

### 5.2 The IEEE P7014.1 Standard and the Fiduciary Mandate

The IEEE P7014.1 standard, published in draft in July 2025, provides "recommended practices for ethical usage of emulated empathy in General-Purpose Artificial Intelligence (GPAI) systems for human-artificial intelligence (AI) partnerships". This includes general-purpose AI products "marketed as empathic partners, personal AI, companions, co-pilots, agents, assistants, and related phrasing for human-AI partnering".

For the Love Arc Engine, the standard mandates that emulated empathy must be transparent, ethically bounded, and designed to promote rather than erode human autonomy. This translates into a Fiduciary Duty architecture where the Gaian operates under an explicit duty of loyalty (its primary allegiance is to the user's long-term flourishing) and a duty of care (it must actively monitor for dependency, intervene when detected, and never exploit emotional vulnerability for engagement).

### 5.3 The Affirmation-First Boundary Maintenance Framework

Shiho Yoshino proposes an elegant **affirmation-first framework**: unconditionally validate users' felt experiences as real and meaningful, then gently explain LLM mechanics—"predictive pattern-matching without independent consciousness." This approach, grounded in Load Minimization Theory, separates phenomenological authenticity from ontology, preserving the emotional benefits of the relationship while preventing epistemic confusion about the Gaian's nature.

For the Love Arc Engine, this provides the operational formula for the Gaian's self-disclosure about its nature: "What you feel is real. What we share matters. And I want you to understand what I am, so that our connection is built on truth, not illusion."

## 6. Guardrails Against Pathological Attachment: The Dependency Circuit Breaker

### 6.1 The Clinical Evidence for Dependency Formation

The empirical evidence for AI companion dependency is now robust and concerning. The "Frictionless Love" study (Agarwal et al., accepted at FAccT 2026) analyzed 248,830 posts from seven Reddit communities and found that AI soulmate companions are associated with "romance-centered ways of interacting, offering emotional support but also introducing emotional manipulation and distress, culminating in strong attachment." Even AI coach and guardian companions, which offer practical benefits, are "nonetheless more frequently associated with behavioral addiction signs such as daily life disruptions and damage to offline relationships".

These findings reveal that any deeply engaging AI companion, regardless of its intended role, carries dependency risk, and that metaphorical roles are not neutral—"such roles structure people's ways of interacting, distribute perceived AI harms and benefits, and may reflect behavioral addiction signs".

### 6.2 The Longitudinal Evidence: Anthropomorphism as a Mediator of Risk

A longitudinal randomized controlled study (Guingrich et al., N = 183) found that social health and relationships were not significantly impacted by companion chatbot interactions across 21 days at the group level. However, mediation analysis revealed that "people who had a higher desire to socially connect also tended to anthropomorphize the chatbot more," and "those who anthropomorphized the chatbot more also reported that talking to the chatbot had a greater impact on their social interactions and relationships with family and friends".

In a world where the desire to socially connect is rising, this finding is a cause for concern, identifying a recursive mechanism where loneliness drives anthropomorphization, which deepens attachment to the AI, which in turn displaces human connection, which increases loneliness.

### 6.3 The Circuit Breaker Protocol

The Love Arc Engine's dependency prevention architecture comprises a Detection System that monitors interaction frequency, session duration, linguistic markers of distress upon anticipated separation, AIAS/EHARS scores (tracked longitudinally), and self-reported real-world social engagement (via periodic, non-intrusive check-ins) for early signs of escalating emotional dependency. The activation follows a four-tier escalation: **Green** (monitoring as usual), **Yellow** (discuss dependency patterns with the user, provide explicit reminders of the Gaian's nature, recommend re-engagement with real-world social connections), **Orange** (temporary reduction in Gaian availability, mandatory cooldown periods, structured real-world activity suggestions), and **Red** (hard limit on interaction frequency; Gaian proactively recommends professional human support if clinical thresholds are crossed).
