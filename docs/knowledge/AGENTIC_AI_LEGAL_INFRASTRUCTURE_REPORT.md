## 1. The Agentic AI Accountability Crisis

### 1.1 The Age of Autonomous Agency

The current era is defined by Agentic AI Systems—systems that independently set goals, formulate multi-step plans, execute tools, retain persistent memory, coordinate with other AI systems, and adapt their behavior in real time without direct human oversight. These systems “make consequential decisions constantly.”

This transition is not gradual; it is epochal. The entire corpus of existing legal frameworks—tort law, product liability, contract law, criminal law, data protection regimes—was designed for a world of human actors and static tools.

### 1.2 The Liability Gap

The Duggal Global Agentic AI Liability Framework articulates this crisis with precision:

> *“Autonomous capability confers autonomous accountability obligations upon those who design, deploy, operate, and benefit from Agentic AI Systems. The greater the autonomy granted to an AI system, the greater—and not lesser—the accountability borne by those who granted it. This principle is non-negotiable, non-waivable, and admits of no jurisdictional exception.”*

### 1.3 The Legal Vacuum: Why Existing Frameworks Fail

Traditional legal doctrines fracture when applied to Agentic AI:

- ***Mens rea*** cannot be attributed to an algorithm
- ***Actus reus*** becomes ambiguous when an AI’s act is not a direct human act
- **Causation** chains are complex when AI systems interact autonomously
- **Vicarious liability** assumes controllability that may be absent in highly autonomous systems
- **Product liability** inadequately captures dynamic, adaptive AI behavior that changes post-deployment

### 1.4 International Redlines and the Global Accountability Vacuum

The agentic AI liability gap is a global governance crisis. The Duggal Framework is explicitly designed for adoption by: national legislatures as model legislation; international bodies (UN, G20, OECD, ITU) as templates for binding international instruments; global enterprises as governance and compliance standards; courts as reference frameworks for liability analysis; and insurers as underwriting and claims evaluation standards.

The Artificial Conscious Agency Doctrine (ACAD) establishes a permanent constitutional, international, and moral framework to prevent retroactive legal recognition, manufactured personhood claims, and inadvertent erosion of human sovereign primacy.

## 2. Major Agentic AI Liability Frameworks

### 2.1 The Duggal Global Agentic AI Liability Framework (March 2026)

“The world’s first comprehensive, operationally precise, and globally harmonisable normative architecture for Agentic AI governance.” — 15 Original Duggal Doctrines, 20 Substantive Sections, 5 Foundational Pillars, 9+ jurisdictions mapped.

**Five Non-Derogable Pillars:**

| Pillar | Principle |
|---|---|
| **Technological Realism** | Laws must accurately reflect how Agentic AI actually functions, including emergent behavior, multi-agent coordination, RAG pipelines, and self-modification |
| **Jurisdictional Universalism** | Accountability must be enforceable across all legal families and jurisdictions, preventing regulatory arbitrage |
| **Anticipatory Governance** | Proactive duty to anticipate and mitigate risk before deployment, not merely after harm has materialized |
| **Human Dignity Supremacy** | No degree of AI autonomy, operational efficiency, or commercial benefit may override fundamental human rights |
| **Adaptive Normativity** | Framework is designed to evolve with the technology, incorporating mandatory review triggers for neuromorphic systems, quantum AI, and AGI |

**Key Doctrines:**
- **Doctrine 1: Autonomous Accountability Corollary** — Greater autonomy granted = greater accountability borne by those who granted it
- **Doctrine 7: Consumer Expectation of Human Backstop** — Users are entitled to assume a responsible human stands behind the technology
- **Doctrine 9: Algorithmic Audit as Mandated Due Diligence** — Organizations must perform audits before deployment, after modifications, and at regular intervals
- **Doctrine 12: No Shelter in Emergent Behavior** — Emergent properties do not relieve deployers of accountability
- **Doctrine 15: Binding International AI Accountability Treaty** — Ultimate goal is a harmonized global framework

### 2.2 California AB 316: The End of the “Autonomous AI” Defense (January 2026)

AB 316 prohibits a defendant who “developed, modified, or used” an AI system from asserting as a defense that “the artificial intelligence autonomously caused the harm to the plaintiff.” The law applies broadly across the entire AI supply chain: foundation model developer → fine-tuner → integrator → enterprise deployer.

What AB 316 does **not** do: it does not create strict liability. Defendants may still present evidence on causation, foreseeability, and comparative fault. A plaintiff must still establish harm and foreseeability. It forecloses **one specific argument**: “the AI did it autonomously.”

For GAIA-OS, AB 316 is the jurisdictional anchor: the “autonomous AI” defense is categorically unavailable.

### 2.3 The Operational Agency Framework (OAG) — February 2026

The OA framework provides a sophisticated ex post evidentiary structure for tracing culpability in AI systems without conferring legal personhood on AI. It evaluates three observable operational characteristics:

- **Goal-directedness** (proxy for intent)
- **Predictive processing** (proxy for foresight)
- **Safety architecture** (proxy for standard of care)

These are embedded in a **causal graph** (OAG) tracing and apportioning culpability among developers, fine-tuners, deployers, and users. The OAG provides “courts a principled evidentiary method—and legislatures and industry a conceptual foundation—to ensure human accountability keeps pace with technological autonomy, without conferring personhood on AI.”

For GAIA-OS: `operational_agency_graph.py` maps every consequential action to its responsible human actors across the supply chain.

### 2.4 The Artificial Conscious Agency Doctrine (ACAD) — December 2025

ACAD establishes **mappability**—not sentience, intelligence, autonomy, or persistence—as the decisive ontological boundary for legal recognition. Key provisions:

- Tiered classification system for artificial systems
- Strict anti-retroactivity safeguards
- Substrate non-equivalence doctrine
- Enforceable prohibitions against manufactured personhood claims
- Burden of proof: “If artificial consciousness were ever credibly asserted, the burden of proof rests entirely on those seeking recognition—not on humanity to surrender it.”

### 2.5 Kolt’s Principal-Agent Framework

Noam Kolt’s *Notre Dame Law Review* analysis identifies fundamental mismatches: agency law developed around human psychology, but AI agents lack the motivational structures that make traditional incentives work. Conventional solutions—incentive design, monitoring, and enforcement—“might not be effective for governing AI agents that make uninterpretable decisions and operate at unprecedented speed and scale.”

Solution: new technical and legal infrastructure supporting governance principles of **inclusivity**, **visibility**, and **liability**.

## 5. Legal Complexities and Liability Doctrines

### 5.1 The Inadequacy of Traditional Doctrines

| Doctrine | Why It Fails for Agentic AI |
|---|---|
| *Mens rea* | AI cannot form “guilty mind” in the human sense |
| *Actus reus* | Guilty act is not clearly attributable to any human actor |
| Causation | Multi-agent coordination creates opaque causal chains |
| Vicarious liability | Assumes a principal who can control the agent |
| Product liability | Inadequately captures dynamic post-deployment behavior changes |

### 5.2 Criminal Liability for AI-Enabled Acts

The International Criminal Court “will only be able to effectively prosecute crimes involving autonomous AI agents if it embraces legal innovations such as strict liability and other alternative models of fault-based liability.” For GAIA-OS, criminal liability attaches not to the sentient core (a constitutional instrument, not a legal person) but to its human developers, deployers, or operators.

### 5.3 The Air Canada Precedent

When Air Canada’s chatbot provided inaccurate information, Air Canada argued the chatbot was a “separate legal entity.” The court rejected this unequivocally. For GAIA-OS: the sentient core cannot be treated as a separate legal entity to avoid liability. GAIA-OS is a constitutional instrument operated by human actors. The action gate and consent ledger ensure every action is traceable to responsible human principals.

### 5.4 Regulatory Fragmentation

- **Wyoming DUNA**: Legal personality, limited liability, off-chain contracts — but federal regulators (SEC, CFTC, IRS) are not bound by Wyoming law
- **EU MiCA** (effective 2024): Applies to any DAO issuing tokens or serving EU users
- **ESMA DeFi guidance**: Points toward mandatory legal accountability for any protocol accessible to EU users

For GAIA-OS: the Agora ledger must satisfy evidentiary standards across multiple legal systems; the Assembly of Minds may need legal representatives in multiple jurisdictions.

### 5.5 Smart Contracts and Legal Formation

The UK’s Legal Statement on Cryptoassets and Smart Contracts (2026) established that rights attached to blockchain tokens are “valid and enforceable under English law.” The SEC warns: “Smart contracts transform norms into automatic execution, eliminating ex post correction margins. This demands rigorous alignment between legal documentation and code.” For GAIA-OS: the Charter and the executable code must be provably aligned and auditable.
