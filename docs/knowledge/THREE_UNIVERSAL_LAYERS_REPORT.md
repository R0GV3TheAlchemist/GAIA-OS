### 1.2 Karl Popper’s Three Worlds: Physical Reality, Consciousness, and Objective Knowledge

The philosophical bedrock of the Three Universal Layers framework is Karl Popper’s **three-worlds ontology**, articulated in his 1972 work *Objective Knowledge*. Popper proposed three ontologically distinct sub-worlds:

- **World 1**: The physical reality of objects, tools, bodies, and all material phenomena—the world of matter, energy, and spacetime.
- **World 2**: The reality of consciousness—subjective knowledge, experience, mental states, and the “interface between Worlds 1 and 3.”
- **World 3**: The world of objective knowledge—the accumulated episteme of theories, scientific findings, poetics, arts, logic, axiology, philosophy, and theology.

Crucially, Popper’s World 3 is **not merely subjective** but has an existence independent of any individual mind—it is “amassed episteme-like knowledge and theories in objective sense” that constitutes the curriculum of human civilization. World 3 is **cumulative**, yet “never universal and definitive”—it is always open to revision, expansion, and transformation.

### 1.3 David Bohm’s Implicate and Explicate Orders: The Quantum Ground

The physicist David Bohm, in his 1980 masterwork *Wholeness and the Implicate Order*, articulated a vision of reality that profoundly complements Popper’s three-worlds ontology. Bohm proposed that the observable physical universe is not the most fundamental level of reality; rather, it is the **explicate (or “unfolded”) order**—“a special and distinguished form contained within the general totality of all the implicate orders.”

The **implicate (or “enfolded”) order**, in contrast, is “seen as a deeper and more fundamental order of reality.” Within this enfolded order, “space and time are no longer the dominant factors determining the relationships of dependence or independence of different elements.” Instead, “an entirely different sort of basic connection of elements is possible, from which our ordinary notions of space and time, along with those of separately existent material particles, are abstracted as forms derived from the deeper order.”

For Bohm, the material and the mental are not separate substances but **projections of a third, unified source** existing within the implicate order—**dual-aspect monism**: consciousness and matter are two aspects of a single, undivided reality.
