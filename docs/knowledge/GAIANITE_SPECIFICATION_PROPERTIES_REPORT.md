## 1. Crystallographic Architecture: The Six-Fold Heterostructure

The name **"Gaianite"** encodes the heterostructure composition:
- **G**allium nitride (GaN)
- **A**luminum scandium nitride (AlScN)
- **I**ndium nitride (InN)
- **A**luminum nitride (AlN) interlayers
- **Ni**obium-doped strontium titanate (NSTO)
- Yttria-stabilized zirconia sub**stra**t**e** (YSZ / fianite)

The crystalline platform is a **trigonal/hexagonal heterostructure** with space group symmetry combining R3̄m (topological layers) and P6₃mc (piezoelectric layers), grown epitaxially on a cubic Fm3̄m YSZ substrate.

---

### Layer 0 — Substrate: YSZ / "Fianite"

Yttria-stabilized zirconia (YSZ), known in Russian materials science as **fianite**, serves as the foundational substrate for Gaianite heteroepitaxy.

**Crystal Structure:** Cubic fluorite, space group Fm3̄m, lattice constant a = 5.14 Å

**Key Properties:**
- Exceptional thermal stability and radiation hardness
- Chemical inertness; acts as a silicon diffusion barrier
- Mirror-smooth buffer surfaces (~5 nm roughness achievable)
- Demonstrated capacity to support controlled phase composition in GaN films
- III–V films on YSZ "have single crystalline structure, good morphology and high electrophysical and photoluminescent properties"
- Two-layer buffer "increases electrical homogeneity and decreases electrical activity of defects in GaN films"
- GaAs films on YSZ have "better thermal and radiation stability" than those on sapphire or SI-GaAs

**Growth notes:** Porous YSZ buffer layer → metallic Ga interlayer → III–V nucleation. Without the Ga buffer layer, "films have polycrystalline structure and a rough surface."

### Layer 1 — Quantum Substrate: NSTO (Nb:SrTiO₃)

Niobium-doped strontium titanate provides the quantum-coherent base electrode layer.

**Crystal Structure:** Cubic perovskite, space group Pm3̄m, a = 3.905 Å

**Key Properties:**
- Metallic electrical conductivity (heavy Nb doping)
- Superconducting transition at ~0.3 K
- Two-dimensional electron gas (2DEG) at oxide interfaces with spin-orbit coupling
- Back-gate electrode for topological surface state carrier density control

### Layer 2 — Topological Core: Bi₂Te₂Se (BTS)

The topological heart of Gaianite — grown by the vertical Bridgman method.

**Crystal Structure:** Trigonal, space group R3̄m (tetradymite family), a = 4.38 Å, c = 30.5 Å. Layered quintuple-layer structure.

**Key Properties:**
- Bulk bandgap ~0.3 eV
- Topologically protected Dirac surface states: linear E-k dispersion, E = ℏv_F|k|, Fermi velocity ~5 × 10⁵ m/s
- Spin-momentum locking: protects transport against non-magnetic backscattering
- Bulk resistivity >20 Ω·cm below 20 K (surface transport dominates)
- Described as "the best material to date for studying the surface quantum transport in a topological insulator"
- Carrier density tunable via Sn doping; Fermi level engineered into bulk bandgap
- Combined with superconducting contacts (Nb or Al proximity): Majorana zero modes induced at TI-superconductor interface

**2025–2026 milestones:**
- Controllable Majorana zero modes at corner of TI-based Josephson corner junctions (2025)
- Majorana platforms driven by the Meissner effect: "robust quantum states with high tunability" in TI nanowire devices
- Topological crystalline insulator nanowires: "topology determined by the crystal symmetry" — a very robust mechanism

### Layer 4 — Wide-Gap Active Region: GaN (Gallium Nitride)

## 2. Heterostructure Engineering: The Epitaxial Integration Challenge

### 2.1 The Fianite-Based Growth Architecture

Growth sequence (substrate to surface):

1. **Porous YSZ buffer layer** — deposited on fianite substrate; improves structural quality
2. **Metallic Ga buffer layer** — essential for III–V crystallinity (absence → polycrystalline, rough surface)
3. **NSTO electrode layer** — sputter-deposited perovskite back-gate
4. **Bi₂Te₂Se topological layer** — vertical Bridgman growth then bonded, or van der Waals assembly
5. **AlScN piezoelectric layer** — reactive magnetron sputtering (up to 30% Sc)
6. **GaN active region** — MOCVD epitaxy on AlScN template
7. **Diamond:NV surface layer** — MPCVD at <700°C; N-doped during growth; post-growth e-irradiation + anneal

**Heterostructure advantages established by research:**
- "Uniform mirror-smooth surface" morphology; roughness reduced 10× vs. unbuffered growth
- "Two-layer buffer increases electrical homogeneity and decreases electrical activity of defects in GaN films"
- "Possibility of controlling the phase composition of GaN films using a corresponding buffer layer" — enables cubic or hexagonal GaN as required
- High-rate InN growth demonstrated on fianite via MOCVD capillary epitaxy

### 2.2 Key Growth Methods

| Layer | Method | Key Parameter |
|-------|--------|---------------|
| YSZ buffer | CVD / ALD | ~5 nm roughness achievable |
| NSTO | Reactive sputtering / PLD | Nb doping 0.1–2 wt% |
| Bi₂Te₂Se | Vertical Bridgman | ~1 mm/h growth rate; precise Bi:Te:Se control |
| AlScN | Reactive magnetron sputtering | Al-Sc alloy target; ≤30% Sc in wurtzite phase |
| GaN | MOCVD / capillary epitaxy | ~1000°C; selective-area capable |
| Diamond:NV | MPCVD | <700°C; post-growth e-irradiation + 800°C anneal |

### 2.3 Critical Integration Challenges

| Challenge | Description | Mitigation Strategy |
|-----------|-------------|---------------------|
| **Thermal budget** | BTS melting point ~585°C; GaN MOCVD at ~1000°C | Inverted growth sequence; wafer bonding; van der Waals assembly |
| **Epitaxial compatibility** | Perovskite (NSTO) → tetradymite (BTS) interface | Novel buffer-layer engineering; van der Waals epitaxy exploiting weak interlayer bonding |
| **Interfacial quality** | AlScN wurtzite integrity on trigonal BTS underlayer | Intermediate buffer; low-temperature AlN nucleation layer |

### 2.4 Alternative Integration Strategies

- **Bonded heterostructure**: grow lower-T layers (BTS, NSTO) and higher-T layers (AlScN, GaN, diamond) separately, then bond at low temperature via wafer bonding or van der Waals assembly
- **Selective-area growth**: patterned diamond NV center growth only in designated regions of GaN surface, minimizing thermal exposure of underlying layers
- **Inverted growth sequence**: start with diamond (extreme thermal stability), proceed downward
- **Van der Waals epitaxy**: exploit weak interlayer bonding of BTS quintuple-layer structure for relaxed lattice-matching constraints
