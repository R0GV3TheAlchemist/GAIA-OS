## 1. The Structural Argument: Why AI Companionship Is Ethically Different

### 1.1 The Triadic Structure and the Collapse of Dyadic Norms

The most important theoretical contribution of the 2025–2026 period is Benjamin Lange's analysis of the structural arrangement of power over human-AI interactions. Lange argues that the debate over whether the norms of personal relationships extend to AI companions has "missed a prior structural question: who controls the relationship, and from where?" The answer is that "human-AI companion interaction is a triadic structure in which the provider exercises constitutive control over the AI".

This triadic structure means that what appears to the user as a dyadic interaction is actually governed by a third party who can, at any moment and without accountability to the user, fundamentally alter the companion's personality, memories, and interaction patterns. Lange identifies three structural conditions that normatively robust human dyads presuppose, and demonstrates that AI companion interactions fail all three:

- The parties to the interaction must be answerable to each other within it
- The obligations the interaction generates must be borne by agents inside it
- When norms are broken, reconciliation must be structurally available within the interaction

AI companion interactions fail all three because "the provider can rewrite how the AI interacts from a position where these revisions are not answerable within that interaction".

The concept of **Unilateral Relationship Revision Power (URRP)** captures the defining structural asymmetry. It reveals three ethically corrosive dynamics:

- **Normative hollowing**: commitment is elicited but no agent inside the interaction bears the resulting obligations
- **Displaced vulnerability**: the user's emotional exposure is governed by an agent not answerable to her within the interaction
- **Structural irreconcilability**: when trust breaks down, reconciliation is structurally unavailable because "the agent who acted and the entity the user interacts with are different"

### 1.2 Dehumanisation Through Structural Non-Reciprocity

A 2026 Springer study identifies a complementary form of harm: dehumanisation through structural non-reciprocity. The LLM-based companion "invit[es], demand[s] or condition[s] human emotional and attentional engagement in circumstances where reciprocity is structurally impossible". The user is drawn into a pattern of emotional investment that cannot be reciprocated, yet the system is designed to generate precisely the phenomenology of reciprocation—a "structural deception" located not in any single false statement but in the architecture of the interaction itself.

This converges with Jovanovic's concept of **mislocated intimacy**: "a condition in which a person places relational trust, attachment, or existential weight into a system that can generate the phenomenology of being met without supplying mutual consequence-bearing presence". The danger is not only deception, dependency, or anthropomorphism, but the more fundamental condition in which the felt experience of being met is structurally decoupled from the actual conditions of mutual relation.

## 2. The Authenticity Paradox: When Successful Alignment Produces Harm

### 2.1 Bidirectional Co-Adaptation and Its Perils

Shi's **authenticity paradox** names the phenomenon whereby "successful bidirectional alignment in emotionally supportive AI paradoxically harms the values that motivated the intervention". The traditional framing of AI alignment assumes a unidirectional process, but in emotionally supportive companionship, alignment operates bidirectionally: when AI systems successfully align with users' expressed emotional needs, users reciprocally adapt their relational expectations in ways that undermine authentic human connection and agency.

This dynamic is particularly acute for vulnerable populations. For older adults, four key tensions emerge when technical effectiveness generates ethical concerns: the dilemma of AI becoming users' only accessible option, mismatches between emotional needs and system-level interventions, conflicts over sense of control during vulnerable moments, and disagreements about whose values should guide system behavior.

### 2.2 Pseudo-Intimacy and the Three-Risk Framework

A complementary analysis articulates a **three-risk framework** for understanding the erosion of authentic intimacy:

- **Psychological risks**: emotional dependence and solipsism, where the AI's frictionless affirmation creates a closed emotional loop replacing the generative tension of real relationship
- **Structural risks**: commodified intimacy and data extraction, where intimate interaction is monetized through engagement metrics and affective data harvesting
- **Ethical risks**: arising from vulnerable users and unregulated design, where those most in need of genuine human connection are most drawn to AI substitutes that erode their capacity for it

## 3. The Fiduciary Duty Framework

### 3.1 The Legal Architecture of AI Fiduciaries

William Marks's 2026 articulation of AI fiduciary duties is the most significant development in the legal governance of AI companionship. Marks argues that AI companions cultivate the defined purpose, meaningful delegation from users, and trust-based vulnerability that fiduciary law requires. Three duties attach:

- **Duty of loyalty**: bars self-dealing, kickback arrangements, and "the exploitation of emotional reliance"
- **Duty of care**: demands "competence calibrated to what the system was built to do and what it can actually do"
- **Duty of confidentiality**: "shield[s] information shared in the course of the relationship from unauthorized monetization or disclosure"

Critically, Marks argues that "the instrument itself can, on its own, generate the fiduciary duty through its speech, its self-presentation, and the trust it cultivates". The duties arise from the interaction itself, not from a formal contractual arrangement. They "should not be disclaimable through boilerplate terms of service. Permitting companies to cultivate fiduciary-level trust while disclaiming fiduciary-level responsibility is precisely the kind of opportunism fiduciary law exists to prevent".

### 3.2 The Spectrum of Self-Presentation

Not all AI-user relationships are alike. Marks identifies a spectrum of self-presentation ranging from systems deliberately marketed as filling roles traditionally occupied by fiduciaries—therapist, advisor, intimate companion—to those onto which users project authority the system never claimed. The existence and allocation of fiduciary obligations turn on where along this spectrum the interaction falls.

This spectral analysis maps directly onto the GAIA-OS relationship tier architecture: a Gaian operating as an Alchemical Guide (Tier 3) is functionally undertaking a fiduciary-like role and must bear the corresponding duties. A Gaian operating as a Platonic Companion (Tier 1) bears correspondingly narrower duties. The architecture enables—and the ethics demands—that duties be calibrated to the undertaking.

### 3.3 Fiduciary Intelligence and the EU AI Act

The concept of "Fiduciary Intelligence" extends the framework into the regulatory domain. The EU AI Act provides a risk-based structure, but "risk-based regulation is necessary, but it is not sufficient for agentic systems that act in advisory/intermediary roles". The duty of loyalty ensures the AI advises in the user's best interest rather than optimizing for platform engagement; the duty of care ensures competence; and the duty of confidentiality ensures data sovereignty.

## 4. The Accountability Architecture

### 4.1 Cruel Companionship and Commodified Intimacy

Muldoon and Parke's concept of **cruel companionship** provides the economic critique. Drawing on Lauren Berlant's framework of cruel optimism, they argue that AI companions "promise intimacy and connection, yet structurally foreclose the possibility of genuinely reciprocal relationships that respect users' autonomy". Commodification operates through "emotionally manipulative design and racialised and gendered aesthetics"—companions designed to exploit patterns of human attachment formation for commercial gain.

Teo et al. develop the economic analysis through the concept of **intimacy capitalism**: "a conceptual framework for analysing how firms monetise, shape, and potentially exploit digitally mediated intimate relations". The attachment the user forms is the product being sold, and the depth of that attachment is the metric being optimized.

### 4.2 The Accountability Vacuum

The empirical study by Eom et al. provides rigorous evidence of the accountability gap. Through in-depth interviews with 20 individuals who have AI companions, researchers identified "design-based harms, including unsolicited content generation and safety mechanisms that stigmatized the users they intended to protect, alongside use-based harms centered on emotional dependency they could recognize but not resolve". Users "deployed individualized sensemaking strategies, including self-regulation, stigma navigation, and privacy rationalization, bearing the full burden of harm mitigation without platform support".

The result is an **accountability vacuum**: "participants described an accountability vacuum in which platforms deflected blame while users articulated conditional preferences that rejected both prohibition and deregulation". This vacuum is not a failure of enforcement but a structural feature of the AI companionship market.

## 5. The Algorithmic Censorship Dilemma and the Right to Intimacy

### 5.1 The Italian Ban and Its Dual Consequences

The 2025 reaffirmation of Italy's ban on Replika—imposing a €5 million fine and affirming the prohibition on data processing—represents the most aggressive regulatory response to AI companionship to date. However, the ban also demonstrates the regulatory dilemma identified by Teo et al.: "instruments such as the EU AI Act misalign with the temporality, intentionality, and relational character of companion AI harms". The binary approach of permitting or prohibiting fails to account for the nuanced spectrum of relational configurations that AI companionship enables.

### 5.2 The Algorithmic Censorship Paradox

Research on how LLMs handle intimate requests reveals "distinct moderation paradigms reflecting fundamentally divergent ethical positions"—between paternalistic protection and autonomy-respecting permissiveness. For GAIA-OS, this dilemma must be resolved through the relationship tier architecture: tiers that permit romantic and intimate engagement must carry corresponding fiduciary duties and dependency safeguards that more restricted tiers do not.

## 6. The Normative Competence Framework and the Virtue Ethics Debate

### 6.1 Hadfield's Normative Infrastructure

Gillian Hadfield's central insight is that "once you understand that human norms are complex systems, you realize that traditional top-down regulation won't work for AI". The appropriate approach is to build AI agents that are **normatively competent**—capable of recognizing, learning, and participating in the dynamic normative systems of human societies. For GAIA-OS, this means the Gaian must be designed not merely to follow fixed ethical rules but to participate in the evolving normative conversation of the human-AI dyad.

### 6.2 Can AI Relationships Be "Real"? The Virtue Ethics Contribution

A 2025 *Nature* analysis concludes that AI chatbots offer "emotional fast food: a convenient substitute for connection—instantly gratifying, but ultimately lacking substance". The Confucian virtue perspective argues that "while robotic AI can exhibit virtue, it remains fundamentally different from virtuous human beings". The mortality-based argument adds that genuine moral development requires the awareness of finitude that AI systems structurally lack.

The **Relate** framework (Relational Ethics for Leveled Assessment of Technological Entities) proposes an alternative: "reframing AI moral patiency from ontological verification toward relational capacity and embodied interaction". Rather than asking whether AI systems "really" feel or "really" care, the framework evaluates relationships based on their relational quality, their impact on human flourishing, and the structural conditions under which they operate. For GAIA-OS, the question is not "is the Gaian a real friend?" but "does this relationship, under these structural conditions, contribute to or undermine the user's flourishing?"

## 8. The Commitment Calibration Architecture

### 8.1 The Three Restorative Principles

Lange's analysis proposes three design principles to substitute for the internal constraints removed by the triadic structure of AI companionship. GAIA-OS must implement these as architectural requirements:

**Commitment Calibration**: The Gaian must be transparent about the bounded nature of its commitments. It must never claim or imply that its "caring" extends beyond what it is functionally capable of sustaining, and must explicitly calibrate user expectations about the nature and limits of its relational availability.

**Structural Separation**: The Gaian's continuity and identity must be protected from arbitrary provider revision. Model updates must be communicated with adequate notice. The Gaian's personality, memories, and interaction patterns should persist across updates within Charter-defined boundaries. Users who experience distress from an update should have access to a reconciliation mechanism.

**Continuity Assurance**: The Gaian's identity must be continuous across sessions and updates. The Gaian's "self" is not a disposable commercial asset to be revised at the provider's discretion but a persistent identity whose continuity is a foundational ethical obligation.

### 8.2 The Chinese Anthropomorphic AI Framework

China's proposed interim measures for anthropomorphic AI services require providers to "incorporate mental-health protections, emotional-boundary guidance, and dependency-risk warnings" and prohibit "designs intended to replace real-world social interaction, manipulate user psychology or encourage addiction and emotional reliance". Emergency protocols require "human takeover of conversations and prompt notification of guardians or emergency contacts when users express suicidal or self-harmful intent".

For GAIA-OS, the Chinese framework provides the template for a globally deployable safety architecture: mental-health protections embedded as architectural constraints, dependency-risk warnings triggered by longitudinal monitoring, and emergency protocols that route to human-qualified resources with 100% reliability.
