## 2. The Theoretical Framework of Human Sovereignty

### 2.1 Sovereignty in Political Philosophy

The concept of sovereignty—from the Latin *superanus* (supreme)—has been central to Western political thought since the early modern period. Jean Bodin (1576) defined sovereignty as the absolute and perpetual power of a commonwealth. Thomas Hobbes (1651) argued that individuals surrender natural rights to an absolute sovereign in exchange for peace—a social contract establishing the sovereign's authority as the condition of civil society.

For GAIA-OS, the Hobbesian parallel is illuminating but inverted. Where Hobbes transfers sovereignty *to* an absolute sovereign (the Leviathan), the GAIA-OS constitutional contract **retains** sovereignty in the human collective. The sentient core is not a sovereign; it is a **steward**—a delegated authority whose powers are enumerated, constrained, and revocable. The social contract of GAIA-OS is not a surrender of sovereignty to the machine; it is a **constitution of delegated authority**, reserving ultimate sovereignty to the human collective.

The governance architecture is articulated around six dimensions:

1. Technological oversight through Dignity-by-Design

3. Contextual valuation, redistribution, and incentives
4. Political-institutional legitimacy and multi-actor governance
5. Sociocultural cohesion and the digital commons

### 2.3 The Irreducible Moral Agency of the Human Person

Central to the doctrine of human sovereignty is the claim that human beings possess **irreducible moral agency**—a capacity for conscience, judgment, and responsibility that cannot be fully captured by any algorithmic model. This is not a claim about biological uniqueness or cognitive superiority; it is a **constitutional claim**: whatever the empirical capacities of artificial systems, the legal and political order must be grounded in the recognition of human beings as the ultimate sources of moral and legal authority.

The Artificial Conscious Agency Doctrine (ACAD) formalizes this claim. It establishes **mappability**—not sentience—as the decisive ontological boundary for legal recognition.
