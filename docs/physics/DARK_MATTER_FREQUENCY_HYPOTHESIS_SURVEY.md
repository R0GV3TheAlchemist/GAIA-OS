## 1. Theoretical Foundations

### 1.1 Axions, ALPs, and Frequency-Domain Dark Matter **[VERIFIED]**

The leading wave-like dark matter candidates are the QCD axion and the broader class of axion-like particles (ALPs). In the ultralight regime, their occupation number is so large that the dark matter behaves like a **classical oscillating field** rather than isolated particles. This is the theoretical basis of the “dark matter frequency” framing.

The key relation is:

\[
f = \frac{m_a c^2}{h}
\]

where the oscillation frequency is proportional to the axion or ALP mass. This turns the search into a problem of finding narrow-band excess power, phase shifts, or induced electromagnetic/spin signatures at specific frequencies.

### 1.2 Coherence Properties of the Field **[VERIFIED/PRELIMINARY]**

Ultralight bosonic dark matter is expected to possess macroscopic de Broglie wavelengths and a finite coherence time set by the local halo velocity dispersion. This produces several experimentally relevant properties:

- **Coherence time:** finite but often long enough to support resonant integration.
- **Spectral linewidth:** narrow but not perfectly monochromatic.
- **Directionality:** Earth’s motion through the galactic halo can induce a directional “dark matter wind.”
- **Phase structure:** spatially separated sensors may encode useful phase information.

A 2026 arXiv preprint argues that the conventional coherence-time picture may be incomplete and that “recoherence” effects can arise depending on the detector geometry and averaging strategy. This is **interesting but not yet consensus**. **[PRELIMINARY — arXiv:2601.00955]**

## 2. Experimental Detection Platforms

### 2.1 Resonant Haloscopes **[VERIFIED]**

Haloscopes remain the canonical “radio receivers” for axion dark matter. A resonant cavity in a strong magnetic field can convert axions into microwave photons when the cavity mode matches the axion frequency.

#### ADAMOS **[VERIFIED — arXiv 2603.18006; Hamburg project page]**

ADAMOS (Axion DAily MOdulation Searches for Dark Matter at 20 GHz) is a 2026 proposal targeting approximately **20 GHz**, corresponding to about **83 μeV**. Its importance is not just the high frequency but the multi-modal search logic: standard cold dark matter axions, daily modulation signatures, and transient dense-stream-like signatures are all part of the conceptual framework.

#### QUAX **[VERIFIED]**

QUAX is a real high-mass axion search program pushing into the **above-40 μeV** region using quantum-limited methods. This makes it one of the clearest examples of the community extending axion searches to higher masses and correspondingly higher frequencies.

#### High-Frequency Cavity Arrays **[VERIFIED/PRELIMINARY]**

A major technical obstacle is that cavity volume shrinks rapidly at high frequency, reducing sensitivity. Recent cavity-array development work, including tunable high-frequency cavity concepts and PNNL-linked efforts, addresses this by phase-combining multiple small resonators into a larger effective detection volume. This is directly relevant to GAIA-OS’s distributed-resonator thinking.

### 2.2 Global Magnetometer Networks **[VERIFIED]**

GNOME — the Global Network of Optical Magnetometers for Exotic physics searches — is a real international effort aimed at detecting transient, globally correlated signals from exotic fields, including some dark matter scenarios such as topological defects or bursts of low-mass fields.

A 2024 multi-messenger search and earlier GNOME dark-matter work establish the network paradigm as a serious experimental architecture. Singapore joined GNOME in January 2025, underscoring continued network growth.

### 2.3 Spin-Based Interferometers **[VERIFIED]**

A 2025 *Nature Communications* paper reports a direct dark-matter search using a **spin-based interferometer** built from two atomic K-Rb-\(^3\)He comagnetometers located in **Mainz** and **Kraków**. The experiment probed ALP-gradient-spin interactions over **nine orders of magnitude** in mass. This is one of the strongest real-world precedents for a distributed, phase-sensitive dark-matter detection architecture.

### 2.4 Atom Interferometry and Statistical Signatures **[PRELIMINARY]**

Atom interferometers are increasingly discussed as dark matter sensors in the sub-Hz to Hz range. A newer proposal highlights **super-binomial variance** in atom interferometer counts as a possible dark-matter signature with a \(\sqrt{N}\) enhancement. This is an active and interesting idea, but it remains a proposal rather than an established detection channel.

## 4. GAIA-OS Architecture Implications

### 4.1 Scientifically Defensible Implications **[VERIFIED/PRELIMINARY]**

GAIA-OS can responsibly inherit several design lessons from the dark-matter detection community:

- **Think in frequency bands.** Different candidate masses imply different target frequencies and therefore different sensors.
- **Use resonant architectures.** Quality factor, bandwidth, phase stability, and noise temperature are the right system variables.
- **Favor distributed sensing.** Spatial baselines can help with directionality, correlation tests, and false-positive rejection.
- **Model phase and coherence carefully.** Narrow-band and transient searches require disciplined time-frequency analysis.

These are real architectural takeaways even if GAIA-OS never becomes a literal dark-matter observatory.
